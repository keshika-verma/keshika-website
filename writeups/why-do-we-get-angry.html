Why Do We Get Angry?

Anger is a complex emotion we all struggle to deal with. Have you ever felt so angry that you felt like screaming or throwing things away just because something did not go your way?

Maybe your sibling ate your share of chocolate, your friend did not return the book they took from you, or the last season of your favourite series did not live up to your expectations.

Imagine yourself walking on the street, amazingly dressed for your friend’s birthday party, when a car swiftly cuts you off and splashes mud on your dress. Suddenly, you turn from a peace-loving creature into the Incredible Hulk.

Changes begin to happen in your body. The amygdala, a part of your brain, releases chemicals that prepare you for intense physical activity. Hormones like adrenaline are also released into the body, causing your heartbeat to increase.

Very fortunately, another part of our brain called the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for making judgments, also gets activated. It helps make sure that we do not react illogically.

Constant chronic anger can significantly increase our chances of getting heart diseases such as high blood pressure, heart attack, or even a stroke. Although it is completely okay to get mad at times, we must always keep our emotions in check and adopt a healthy lifestyle.

Discussing our anger with an adult, such as a teacher or parent, can often help it go away. To manage anger better, one can also practice meditation and breathing exercises regularly.
