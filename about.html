I’m Keshika Verma, a student, builder, writer, and someone who cares deeply about using ideas, technology, and leadership to do meaningful work.

This page is not just a summary of what I have done. It is a window into who I am, where I come from, what has shaped me, and what I hope to build going forward.

To God be the Glory.

Who I Am

Hi, I’m Keshika. I’m someone who has always been deeply curious, about people, about the world, about the future, and about how ideas can actually be turned into things that matter. Over time, that curiosity found expression through many different forms: coding, writing, public speaking, leadership, research, education, and social impact work.

I do not see these as separate parts of my life. To me, they are all connected. Writing helps me think. Technology helps me build. Leadership helps me bring people together. Service reminds me why any of it matters in the first place.

I am an aspiring computer scientist and writer, but more than that, I am someone trying to make full use of the opportunities I have, while also creating new ones for others wherever I can.

Where I Come From

I come from the Himalayan region, and that has shaped me more deeply than I can explain in a single paragraph. Growing up around mountains gives you a strange combination of grounding and longing. You are constantly surrounded by beauty, but you also experience a lack of opportunity.

As a child, I was curious about almost everything. I loved learning, creating, and exploring different interests. Alongside academics, I was deeply involved in extracurriculars like dancing, singing, painting, and many other activities that allowed me to express myself creatively. Dance, in particular, was something I loved intensely. It was a space where energy, discipline, and joy came together. Those experiences made me an all-rounder in many ways, someone who enjoyed growing through both intellectual and creative pursuits.

At the same time, computers fascinated me. They felt like windows into another world. While I was exploring creative activities, I was also increasingly drawn toward technology and programming. But access was limited, and advanced exposure to technology was not something that naturally existed around me. So I began learning in the way many deeply curious people do, piece by piece, from whatever I could find. I explored free resources, tutorials, ideas, and slowly began teaching myself what I could.

That journey taught me two things very early: first, that curiosity can travel farther than circumstance; and second, that access changes lives. Both of those truths still guide me.

Growth

My journey did not happen all at once. It unfolded step by step, often in ways I did not fully understand at the time.

The first moment that made me believe in my academic potential came in Grade 7, when I stood first in my class for the first time. It was a small milestone, but it gave me confidence that consistent effort could truly change outcomes.

Soon after, the world changed. The pandemic and lockdown disrupted the rhythm of everyday life for everyone. But instead of slowing down, that period became a time of exploration and growth for me. With more time to learn independently, I began developing new skills, discovering new interests, and pushing myself further than I had before.

Over time, I also developed a deep love for reading and writing. Books became more than stories to me. They became worlds, ideas, and possibilities. By the time I was in Grade 8, at just twelve years old, I had started writing my own novel. That early project was the beginning of my journey as a writer. I ultimately published my debut novel, The Last Enemy: One World - One Fate, in Grade 12. More about the book later in the story.

At the same time, I remained deeply involved in creative activities like dancing, which had always been an important part of my life. Creativity, academics, and curiosity were all growing together during those years.

During this period, I also became increasingly interested in computer science. My brother was already working in the field, and being around that environment helped me understand the possibilities technology offered. Naturally, I began exploring programming myself and even started Harvard’s CS50x course, where I spent many hours learning and experimenting with code.

Alongside these interests, leadership also became an important part of my school life. I served as Vice Head Girl in Grade 9 and later as Head Girl in Grade 10, roles that allowed me to represent my peers and contribute to the school community in meaningful ways.

Public speaking and literature were also areas where I found great joy. I represented my school three times at the ASISC literary events in Grades 8, 9, and 10, winning a prize each time. In Grade 8, I advanced to the regional level and secured third place in North India. In Grade 9, I placed first in my state and again represented my region at the North India level. In Grade 10, I secured second place in the state. I also won a laptop in a speaking competition organized by Byju’s, an ed-tech company.

Academically, I continued to perform strongly across subjects and often ranked first in various school quizzes and academic competitions. In my Grade 10 ICSE board examinations, I secured 96.4%, standing first in my school and likely becoming the city topper as well.

That year was particularly meaningful for many reasons. I had the opportunity to host a major cultural event attended by over 1,950 people at Atal Sadan, Kullu-Manali, an experience that strengthened my confidence and love for being on stage.

In recognition of my overall performance in academics, leadership, and extracurricular activities, I was awarded the Neon Dhairya Sharma Award, the highest honor given by my school and its equivalent of the Student of the Year award. I received it alongside my proud parents, a moment I will always remember.

During the same period, I was also crowned Miss OLS at my school, Our Lady of the Snows High School, an award that recognized not just academic and extracurricular achievements, but also overall participation and presence within the school community.

Looking back, those years were not just about achievements. They were about discovering who I was becoming, someone who loved learning, creating, leading, and constantly growing.

I Moved to Chandigarh in 2024

In 2024, in search for more opportunities, I moved to Chandigarh to begin a new chapter of my education, and almost immediately, my life began changing in ways I had never expected.

Within the first two weeks of joining the school, I was appointed Disaster Management In-Charge, a responsibility that required coordination, preparedness, and leadership in critical situations. Around the same time, I also joined the National Service Scheme (NSS) as a volunteer, where I began engaging in community service initiatives and learning the importance of social responsibility.

What started as a new environment soon became a year full of exploration. I began taking up online internships to challenge myself beyond the classroom. I joined Unsway, a start-up recognised by, Crimson Education, Government of India, BITS Hyderabad, Hansraj College, where I initially served as an Outreach Coordinator for the Coding Club. Over the span of several months, my work and dedication led to multiple recognitions, including Employee of the Month awards, and I was eventually promoted to become the Director of the Coding Club. During this time, I also worked as a Creative Content Writer at Saarthi: My Mental Health Companion, contributing to initiatives aimed at spreading awareness and support around mental health.

During Grade 11, I was also admitted to the Indigo Research NextGen IRIS Program and received a scholarship of approximately USD $456. Although I ultimately could not attend the program, receiving the admission and scholarship itself was meaningful because it reflected my growing interest in research, writing, and academic exploration beyond the school curriculum.

That year also brought some remarkable academic and leadership opportunities. I secured All India Rank 6 in the Bal Education Trust National Debate Competition, an experience that strengthened my love for public speaking and critical thinking.

Around the same time, I helped organize SHS MUN and the Leaders Conclave, one of the largest student conferences in North India. Hosted at Sacred Heart Senior Secondary School, Chandigarh, the conference brought together over 1,000 delegates for the Model United Nations and more than 300 participants for the Leaders Conclave, representing 10+ cities across the region. I served as a compère and Assistant Head of Department, helping coordinate and lead the event while managing large-scale logistics and discussions across multiple committees.

Another important milestone that year was being selected as a Peer Educator under the Adolescent Peer Educators Leadership Program, an initiative run by the Ministry of Education in collaboration with the Central Board of Secondary Education (CBSE). This role allowed me to work closely with students, spreading awareness about well-being, health, and personal development while helping create a supportive environment within the school. Working with the Central Board of Secondary Education and Ministry of Education was the biggest thing for me.

However, Grade 11 was not only a year of achievements. It was also a year of intense personal struggle.

During this time, I went through one of the most difficult mental health phases of my life. I was dealing with severe anxiety, frequent panic attacks, and a period of depression that deeply affected my ability to function the way I normally would. The pressure of a new city, new expectations, and constant responsibility often felt overwhelming.

But rather than hiding these struggles, I chose to confront them openly.

At the SHS Leaders Conclave, I delivered a mock TED-style talk where I spoke about the challenges I was experiencing and the ways I was trying to work through them. I shared the coping mechanisms I was learning, the habits I was building, and the importance of acknowledging mental health struggles rather than ignoring them. Speaking about it publicly was both difficult and empowering, and it became an important moment in my personal journey.

Later that year, in December, I experienced what became one of the most transformative experiences of my life.

I was selected to attend a seven-day residential bootcamp at the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Delhi, organized by The Tale of Humankind. Living and learning on the IIT campus for a week exposed me to an incredible environment of innovation, collaboration, and creative problem solving.

During the bootcamp, I worked with a team to develop two AI-based assistive technology prototypes:
1. WaveSync, designed as assistive technology for individuals with hearing impairments
2. Optipulse, an assistive solution aimed at supporting visually impaired individuals

The experience pushed me to think about how technology can be used not just to innovate, but to create solutions that genuinely improve people’s lives.

Another personal milestone during this year was building my very first website using HTML and CSS.

It was my first real step into web development and the beginning of a much deeper interest in technology and digital creation.

Looking back, Grade 11 was not simply a year of accomplishments or setbacks. It was a year that tested my resilience, pushed me out of my comfort zone, and forced me to grow emotionally, intellectually, and personally.

It was the year I learned that growth is rarely linear, and that sometimes the hardest years shape you the most.

12th, The Most Happening Year of My Life

Grade 12 became the most intense and defining year of my life. I entered the year carrying the same mental health struggles that had begun earlier. Anxiety, panic attacks, and overwhelming emotional pressure were constant companions. I was undergoing therapy, trying to understand and manage what I was experiencing, but progress was slow and often frustrating. There were many moments when simply focusing on everyday life felt extremely difficult.

Yet despite this internal battle, life outside continued to move forward in remarkable ways. Because of the work I had done during Grade 11, I was appointed as the Discipline Executive of the school, one of the most respected and selective leadership positions in the senior cabinet. What made the moment even more surprising was that I had joined the school only a year earlier. Being entrusted with such responsibility so quickly was both humbling and deeply motivating.

Towards the beginning of Grade 12, I received a research opportunity from the Cambridge Centre for International Research (CCIR). I was admitted to the CCIR Future Scholar's Program, where I was offered the opportunity to research with professors and PhD candidates from the University of Cambridge, Imperial College London, and the University of Oxford.

The topic I was selected for was “Building Better AI: Investigating Errors in Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT.” This felt especially meaningful because it connected directly with my growing interest in artificial intelligence, research, and the way modern technology makes mistakes, learns, and affects real people. I also received the prestigious CCIR STEM Scholarship for the same.

Around the same period, I was also admitted to Mulai Coding’s CS10 course with a fee waiver. For me, this was another sign that I was slowly moving from simply being interested in computer science to actively building a stronger foundation in it.

Grade 12 was also the year of standardized tests and international applications. I scored an 8.0 band out of 9.0 in IELTS, which became an important milestone in my application journey. In the SAT, I scored in the 94th percentile worldwide, with 700+ scores in both English and Mathematics across my attempts.

Because of this result, I was also selected as an SAT tutor on Schoolhouse.world, an official SAT tutoring platform supported by the College Board and Khan Academy. Being trusted to tutor other students felt special because it turned my own preparation into something useful for others too.

Writing continued to remain a major part of my identity as well. I received a Special Mention in the Queen’s Commonwealth Essay Competition, an international achievement that meant a lot to me because it recognized my voice as a young writer beyond my own school and city.

I also made a conscious effort to stay updated with the rapidly changing world of artificial intelligence. I attended the Generative AI Mastermind course by Outskill, a 16-hour program conducted over two days to help learners become AI generalists. The mentors included Vaibhav Sisinty, Shantanu Tungare, and Phani Krishna. It helped me understand how AI tools, workflows, and prompting could be used more practically and creatively.

I also participated in Budget Quest and cleared one round of it. It was not the biggest achievement of the year, but it added to my interest in public policy, finance, and national-level learning opportunities.

During this time, I also reached a milestone that had been years in the making. On 25 March 2025, at 3 in the morning, I finally published my first novel, a science fiction book I had begun writing in Grade 8.

The journey to publication had not been linear. There were pauses during my board examinations and again during the difficult period in Grade 11, but the story stayed with me throughout those years. When the book was released, it received an incredible response and secured Rank #2 in the Kindle Bookstore in the Science Fiction category within the first week. Seeing a dream that began at age twelve become real was one of the most surreal moments of my life.

At the same time, I continued exploring research and technology. Inspired by the recurring floods in my home region in Himachal Pradesh, I conducted independent research on Automation of Spillway Gates of Dams and Barrages. The project proposed an automated system capable of monitoring reservoir levels and adjusting spillway gates in real time to prevent panic-driven decisions during flooding seasons. The research was later presented to the Himachal Pradesh Power Corporation Limited, which appreciated the concept and expressed interest in exploring such automation in the future. The project was deeply personal to me because the floods had affected my own community in the Himalayas.

While working on this research, I also began studying broader questions around climate action and sustainability. Understanding the relationship between environmental systems, infrastructure, and technology became a growing interest, and it shaped the way I began thinking about the role of innovation in solving real-world problems.

Another initiative that meant a great deal to me was the launch of the Digital Disha Program. After months of planning, I conducted the first cohort of this digital literacy initiative in a government school in the Himalayan region. The program was designed to introduce students in rural communities to computers, digital tools, and the broader possibilities of technology. Initially, there was significant resistance because many families were unfamiliar with computers and hesitant to adopt new technologies. Through conversations, demonstrations, and patience, I was able to run the program successfully, helping students take their first steps into the digital world.

I had become a "FOUNDER"! And I hope to keep working and taking Digital Disha forward. Digital literacy is important to me, and I want to give more students an opportunity to become digitally literate.

While working with students during Digital Disha, I realized that many of them struggled with something very basic: typing. This observation eventually led me to begin working on a startup idea. I, with my co-founder, Alankrit Verma, launched KeyZen, an AI-powered typing tutor designed to help users improve typing speed and accuracy through personalized drills, real-time coaching, and performance analytics. Today, I am the Co-founder, COO and Head of Product of KeyZen.

During the same period, I also had the opportunity to interact with Dr. Kiran Bedi, India’s first woman IPS officer and former Lieutenant Governor of Puducherry. I shared my experience with Digital Disha and the challenges of bringing technology awareness to rural communities. Hearing her encouragement for the initiative was incredibly inspiring and reinforced my belief that meaningful change often begins with small, determined steps.

Another major milestone came when I was selected to participate in the Viksit Bharat Young Leaders Dialogue (VBYLD) 2026 at Bharat Mandapam, New Delhi. The selection process involved a rigorous four-stage evaluation: a national quiz, an essay round, a presentation submission, and a final interview. The event had received 50 lakh+ applications, and being chosen to represent Chandigarh through this process was a proud moment. The event itself was extraordinary, as I had the rare opportunity to witness and attend a session with the Prime Minister of India alongside other major leaders and entrepreneurs from across the country.

Even during the most stressful moments, curiosity never left me. In my free time, I explored concepts in electronics and physics, eventually building a full-wave rectifier circuit from scratch. Small experiments like these kept reminding me why I loved learning in the first place.

However, this year was also marked by a deeply personal loss. My grandfather passed away during this time, and his death affected me profoundly. I was already navigating significant mental health challenges, and losing someone so close to me intensified those emotions. It was one of the hardest periods of my life.

Despite everything happening internally and externally, I continued moving forward, applying to universities in Canada and the United States, completing projects, leading initiatives, and preparing for the next stage of my life.

Even while navigating applications, final exams, projects, and everything else happening around me, I continued trying to grow as a developer. During this time, I was selected as a contributor at GirlScript Summer of Code, an open-source program that gave me a way to begin contributing to real projects, understand collaborative development, and step into the open-source community.

For me, this mattered because it showed that I was not only learning computer science for admissions or academics. I was slowly beginning to participate in the larger developer world, one contribution at a time.

My school farewell took place on 31 January, marking the symbolic end of my school journey (I got crowned Ms. Erudite ❤️), and my final board examination was on 7 April. With that final paper, one of the most eventful and transformative chapters of my life came to a close.

Looking back, Grade 12 was not simply a year of achievements. It was a year that tested my resilience, deepened my empathy, and forced me to grow stronger than I had ever imagined.

The end of school life.
16 years old.
I loved it ❤️

After 12th: The College Admissions Season

After the end of school, another intense chapter began: the college admissions process. It was a season filled with applications, decisions, scholarship letters, waiting, uncertainty, and finally, moments that made the entire journey feel real.

The biggest milestone was my admission to the University of Waterloo for Honours Computer Science, Co-op. Getting into Waterloo Computer Science felt especially meaningful because it brought together so many things I had been working toward: technology, problem-solving, ambition, and the dream of learning in one of the strongest computer science and co-op ecosystems in the world.

At the same time, I also appeared for college admissions in India and received offers from well-known private universities for computer science and engineering programs. These included Kalinga Institute of Industrial Technology (KIIT), Bhubaneswar for Computer Science with Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning, SRM Institute of Science and Technology at the Kattankulathur main campus for Computer Science and Engineering with Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning, UPES Dehradun for B.Tech Computer Science and Engineering, and Amrita University for a B.Tech program.

I also had offers from other Indian universities such as Chitkara University, Chandigarh University, and Lovely Professional University for Computer Science and Engineering. But the direction that felt most aligned with my long-term goals was clear: I wanted a computer science education that would give me strong academics, real-world exposure, and room to keep building.

Looking back, the admissions season was not just about getting accepted into universities. It was about seeing years of curiosity, effort, leadership, writing, resilience, and building finally open doors across countries.

What Drives Me

What drives me most is the desire to grow meaningfully and contribute meaningfully.

I care about building things that are not just impressive, but genuinely useful, whether that is through technology, education, leadership, or creativity.

I am also deeply shaped by struggle and reflection. Working through anxiety and intense pressure changed the way I see success, ambition, and people.

It made me more self-aware, more resilient, and more empathetic, and reminded me that real achievement should never come at the cost of losing yourself.

What I’m Building Now

Right now, I am focused on building things that connect technology with real human impact.

Through Digital Disha, I work to make digital literacy more accessible for students in rural and less connected communities.

I am also building KeyZen, an AI-powered typing tutor inspired by a simple but important gap I noticed while teaching: digital opportunity begins with digital fluency.

Alongside that, I continue to write, explore ideas, and grow into the kind of builder and thinker I hope to become.
